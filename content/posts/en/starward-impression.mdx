I played a game called Starward, so here are my thoughts on it!

[Official website](https://xzyen.shengtiangames.com/)

This game follows the vs.-series battle system that Gundam has run in arcades for roughly 20 years now.

I was in junior high and high school back when "Mobile Suit Gundam: Gundam vs. Gundam Next" and the original "Mobile Suit Gundam Extreme Vs." were around, and I used to go to the arcade with friends after school to play them.

It's fair to say that series is the game of my youth, and I have a lot of attachment to it.

High-fiving my friends when we won at the arcade, watching the replay together when we lost... just remembering it makes something well up in my chest. Those days aren't coming back... ( ；∀；)

So, going into this game — which I don't think it's unfair to call a "clone" of the game of my youth —

**I was moved!!!! It's so much fun!!!!!!!!**

Below I'll ramble on about why.

(From here on, I'll refer to the EXVS series simply as EXVS.)

## The characters feel fresh and fun

Honestly, EXVS's mobile suits have had roughly the same looks and performance for about 10 years now, so I'd gotten a bit tired of them. ( ・∇・)

Starward, on the other hand, has cute girls, and each unit's performance feels new too (even though plenty of it is clearly an homage to EXVS), so **playing it genuinely feels fresh and fun.**

And even though the characters and their combat designs are original, it doesn't break down as an EXVS-like game at all. As I'll get into below, this game offers a different kind of fun from EXVS while still being EXVS-like, and it firmly establishes itself as its own thing.

## Shuffle matching rarely results in "accidents"

**This is what impressed me the most! It's a feature I've been wishing for after 20 years of Gundam!!!!!**

### The problem with playing Gundam via shuffle matching

First, this game uses the familiar cost system.

Each team gets 6 total cost, and characters are assigned one of four costs — "1.5," "2," "2.5," or "3" — and you win by depleting the opponent's 6 cost entirely.

I'll skip the details, but generally, both a 3-cost paired with another 3-cost and a 1.5-cost paired with a 3-cost are called "accidents" — very awkward combinations to deal with.

Honestly, in Gundam, I have to say 3-cost and 1.5-cost characters are hard to use in shuffle matching once you factor in the risk of an accident.

### How matching works in Starward

Starward's matching method goes like this:

1. Pick 4 characters from your own roster.
2. Each side bans one of the opponent's characters, one at a time.
3. Choose from whichever characters weren't banned.

On this third screen where you pick your character... you can check your partner's character while choosing your own!!!

![Matching screen](https://assets.now-imarin.com/posts/starward-impression/starward-impression_2.webp)

This lets you pick your character while factoring in what your partner is using.

This is... genuinely great.

Beyond avoiding the cost-related "accidents" I mentioned above, you can also build a composition that complements your partner.

For example, if your partner is using a 3-cost frontline character, you can decide to go low-cost and play support yourself.

Personally, I feel like picking something equivalent to a 1.5-cost unit in Gundam leads to an accident about 80% of the time, so I always avoided it out of fear of causing trouble for my partner — but in this game you can adapt on the fly.

## About the battle system

### More HP, but a shorter red-lock range

Here's the HP and main shot damage for a typical low-cost character (quoting the wiki):

| Character | HP   | Main Shot | Main Shot / HP |
| --------- | ---- | --------- | -------------- |
| Gundam    | 660  | 158       | 23.9%          |
| Ducalion  | 2268 | 441       | 19.4%          |

What I want to say here is that:

**In Starward, you lose less HP per hit compared to EXVS.**

On the other hand, **Starward's red-lock range is shorter than EXVS's**, so you basically have to close in on the opponent, which tends to result in chaotic close-quarters fights.

Here are my thoughts once you factor that in:

- EXVS
  - Tends to become a tense, drawn-out mid-to-long-range shooting battle.
  - Since a single hit takes off a large chunk of HP, the tension of trading shots is fun.
  - Getting hit even once really hits you emotionally.
- Starward
  - Lots of close-to-mid-range exchanges, and fights tend to turn into a chaotic scramble.
  - Even with strong tracking or firepower, shots that root you in place are hard to use.
  - Support characters can get away fairly easily if they just want to run, but it's hard to actually get work done while doing that.

At a glance it looks like an EXVS-like game with nearly the same system, but once you actually play it, I felt they're quite different: EXVS is a "shoot-'em-up bullet hell," while Starward is a "close-range free-for-all."

### Shielding is a single button press

In EXVS, the shield command is input by pressing the stick "down, then up" — presumably due to constraints of the arcade cabinet's buttons.

**Honestly, it's hard to pull off. ( ・∇・)**

Personally, when I watch gameplay videos and see someone throw up a shield on reflex, I think, "this person is good."

Starward, meanwhile, lets you shield with a single button. In exchange for being cancelable out of various commands, it has a limited number of charges.

**It's so much easier to input!!**

![Keeping track of your remaining shield charges is tricky](https://assets.now-imarin.com/posts/starward-impression/starward-impression_3.webp)

I'm still not used to seeing a "shield" entry in the command list. ( ・∇・)

I imagine this will be divisive among EXVS veterans, but for someone like me who always thought "if only I could use shields well, I'd probably fight a lot better," this was an eye-opening feature.

## Closing thoughts

This game is incredibly fun!

I sincerely hope it becomes more widely popular.

See you next time. ( ・∇・)
